# Open Questions (Flux AI Literacy Framework)

> Quick start: run one scenario for 10–20 minutes, capture (1) student rubric evidence, (2) one re-entry question + one revision, and (3) one verification check trace. Then use 1–3 questions below to evaluate what changed.

This framework is intentionally **auditable in artifacts** (rubric evidence, checks, revision traces). The questions below are not “claims we’ve proven.” They are **evaluation prompts** meant to guide collaborators, reviewers, and teacher educators toward evidence that would strengthen—or falsify—key parts of the framework.

**How to use this file**
- Pick 1–3 questions that match your context.
- Run a short scenario + rubric loop.
- Collect artifacts (student rubric, teacher rubric notes, revisions, checks).
- Note what evidence you saw and what would change your mind.

---

## 1) Interaction-frame drift
**Question:** Does an explicit stance (context partnering) measurably change the interaction frame from “help-me-fast” toward “help-me-learn”?

**Why it matters:** The framework’s core problem statement is that interaction frames drift toward speed/compliance/unearned certainty unless steered.

**What evidence would count:**
- Prompts and responses show more explicit assumptions, boundaries, and uncertainty.
- Learner artifacts include re-entry moves (revision based on a missing move).
- Fewer unverified claims survive into final work.

**Where to look (artifact surface):** revision traces, verification notes, teacher rubric evidence, chat transcripts.

---

## 2) Ownership stays with the learner
**Question:** Do learners remain responsible for meaning (authorship/agency) while using AI, rather than outsourcing authority?

**Why it matters:** If ownership collapses, the framework becomes “better prompting” rather than literacy.

**What evidence would count:**
- Keep/change/reject decisions with reasons.
- Learner can distinguish their thinking from tool output.
- Tool use is disclosed without becoming the authority.

**Where to look:** student rubric evidence, teacher rubric notes, reflection lines, revision rationale.

---

## 3) Verification and evidence actually happen
**Question:** Does the framework increase the frequency and quality of domain-appropriate checks (tests, examples, sources, counterexamples)?

**Why it matters:** “Careful language” can be performative; verification is harder to fake.

**What evidence would count:**
- At least one concrete check is recorded when needed.
- Corrections are made after checking.
- Learner can name what would make the claim fail.

**Where to look:** work annotations, check logs, citations, test cases, counterexamples, correction notes.

---

## 4) Re-entry questions reduce mechanical rubric use
**Question:** Do “common drifts → re-entry questions” help novices facilitate without needing the author in the room?

**Why it matters:** A portable framework must carry interpretive guidance, not just destinations.

**What evidence would count:**
- Facilitators can identify a plausible “missing move” and select a re-entry question.
- Revisions show targeted improvement rather than generic polishing.
- Calibration discussions converge on what counts as evidence.

**Where to look:** teacher-ed calibration notes, facilitator reflections, revised artifacts, group norming outputs.

---

## 5) Progress markers are observable (without becoming a taxonomy)
**Question:** Can facilitators reliably distinguish progress markers (e.g., noticing drift after the fact → diagnosing in real time) using artifacts?

**Why it matters:** The framework aims to support cultivated judgment without turning into levels.

**What evidence would count:**
- Multiple reviewers can point to the same artifact evidence for a given marker.
- Disagreements lead to clearer evidence criteria rather than vibes.

**Where to look:** calibration records, annotated examples, teacher rubric evidence, revision traces.

---

## 6) Transfer across domains
**Question:** Do the same core practices show up when row packs are mixed across subjects (math, writing, science, history)?

**Why it matters:** If practices don’t transfer, the framework becomes domain-bound.

**What evidence would count:**
- Similar ownership/verification/context moves appear in different domains.
- Learners can name the practice independent of the subject.

**Where to look:** mixed-domain row sets, student evidence, teacher rubric notes across tasks.

---

## 7) Integrity & boundaries hold under real classroom constraints
**Question:** Do disclosure, privacy, attribution, and appropriate use remain intact when time pressure and incentives increase?

**Why it matters:** Integrity is where frameworks often fail under stress.

**What evidence would count:**
- Clear disclosure norms and consistent application.
- Learners choose non-AI paths when appropriate.
- No sensitive data leakage; attribution norms followed.

**Where to look:** tool-use notes, classroom policy alignment, teacher rubric evidence, assignment artifacts.

---

## 8) Failure modes: performative “care” vs real evidence
**Question:** Can the framework detect and correct performative epistemic-care (hedging language without checks) and other “looks-like” behaviors?

**Why it matters:** Without this, the framework can be gamed by style.

**What evidence would count:**
- Re-entry prompts steer learners from rhetoric to checks.
- Rubric evidence references concrete verification rather than tone.

**Where to look:** student rubric entries, teacher notes, revisions, check traces.

---

## How you can help as a reviewer
If you’re evaluating this framework, the most helpful contributions are:
- a short run of the loop (scenario → rubric → re-entry → revision),
- the resulting artifacts,
- and notes on which questions above were clarified—or made more confusing—by that run.
